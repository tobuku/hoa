The Sunshine Clause: Your Right to Open HOA Meetings

HOA board meetings must generally be open to all members. Here's what that means, what the valid exceptions are, and what to do when the board operates in the dark.

Quick Win: Request all meeting minutes for the past 2 years. Boards that have been holding secret meetings often have gaps in their minutes — or minutes that are suspiciously vague about what was discussed and decided.

What Is the Sunshine Clause?

The term "Sunshine Clause" refers broadly to open meeting requirements that apply to HOA boards. The concept mirrors government sunshine laws — the idea that decisions affecting a community should be made in the open, with the people affected by those decisions able to observe them.

In the HOA context, this means board meetings — where major decisions about your community are made, budgets are passed, and fines are imposed — are generally required to be open to all members of the association. You have the right to sit in the room and watch your board govern. The board cannot legally conduct the business of your association in private, behind closed doors, without proper notice.

This right is not absolute or universal. It depends on your state law, your HOA's governing documents, and the type of meeting being held. But in a growing majority of states, open meeting requirements for HOA boards have statutory force — meaning the board doesn't just have a moral obligation to be transparent. They have a legal one.

Which States Have Explicit Open Meeting Requirements?

Open meeting laws for HOAs have expanded significantly over the past two decades. States with explicit statutory open meeting requirements for HOA or community association boards include:

  • California — The Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act (Civil Code §4900 et seq.) is one of the most member-protective open meeting statutes in the country. Board meetings must be open, agendas must be posted four days in advance, and members have a right to speak.
  • Florida — Florida Statutes §720.303 requires HOA board meetings to be open to members, with advance notice posted conspicuously in the community.
  • Texas — The Texas Property Code requires open board meetings with proper notice, and members have the right to speak on agenda items.
  • Colorado — The Colorado Common Interest Ownership Act (CCIOA) requires open meetings and advance notice to members.
  • Arizona — A.R.S. §33-1804 requires planned community board meetings to be open, with the agenda available to members in advance.
  • Nevada — NRS Chapter 116 provides some of the country's strongest HOA member protections, including detailed open meeting requirements with strict notice timelines.
  • Virginia — The Virginia Property Owners' Association Act and Condominium Act both require open board meetings.
  • Washington — The Washington Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act (WUCIOA) includes open meeting requirements for covered associations.

Even if your state is not on this list, your HOA's own bylaws may contain open meeting requirements. Check your governing documents before assuming you have no right to attend.

What Members Can and Cannot Do at Open Meetings

Open meeting rights do not make you a voting participant in every board decision. The board runs the meeting. But you have meaningful protections as an observer and, in many states, as a speaker.

What you are generally entitled to do:

  • Attend and observe the meeting
  • Receive proper advance notice of the meeting date, time, and location
  • Review the agenda before the meeting (varies by state)
  • Speak during the member open forum or comment period (required in many states)
  • Record the meeting in most states, unless your HOA's governing documents specifically prohibit it
  • Inspect and receive copies of approved meeting minutes

What you generally cannot do:

  • Interrupt board deliberations or demand the floor during business items
  • Vote on board business (unless it's a membership vote item on the agenda)
  • Compel the board to change an agenda item or add items on the spot (in most states)
  • Require the board to answer your specific questions in real time (though many boards will)

How to Invoke Your Right to Attend

In most cases, invoking your right to attend is simple: you show up. Board meetings are typically noticed in common areas or by mail. If you're not receiving notices, that itself is a problem — notice is usually required by law.

If you've been told by a board member or manager that the meeting is "for the board only" or that you are not welcome, respond in writing. A simple, professional email to the board stating that you intend to exercise your right to attend under [your state statute or your HOA's bylaws] creates a paper trail and often resolves the issue without further conflict.

If your state has a specific open meeting statute, cite it by name. If the right is in your bylaws, quote the relevant section. Make it clear that you are not being combative — you are simply exercising a legal right.

Executive Sessions: What's Valid vs. What's Abuse

Every open meeting law recognizes that some matters cannot be discussed in front of the general membership. These closed sessions are called executive sessions, and they exist for legitimate reasons. Understanding the difference between a valid executive session and one being used to hide board misconduct is critical.

Legitimate grounds for executive session typically include:

  • Legal advice — Discussions with the HOA's attorney, particularly about pending or threatened litigation
  • Active litigation — Strategy discussions about lawsuits in which the HOA is a party
  • Personnel matters — Discussions about HOA employees or contractors (e.g., performance issues, termination)
  • Individual member disciplinary hearings — When a specific member's fine or violation is being reviewed, many states require this be offered privately to protect that member's privacy
  • Contract negotiations — In some states, preliminary discussion of vendor contracts before terms are finalized

Signs that executive session is being abused:

  • The board routinely conducts most of its business in executive session, with little left for the open meeting
  • Actions taken in executive session are not announced or summarized at the reconvened open meeting
  • The board uses "legal advice" as a blanket reason to close any meeting where controversy is expected
  • Major policy decisions — fee increases, rule changes, special assessments — are "discussed" in executive session before being rubber-stamped at the open meeting
  • No record of executive session topics is kept, even in general terms
"Executive session is a tool to protect sensitive information — not a shield to hide governance from the people being governed. When boards use it as the latter, they are often violating both the spirit and the letter of open meeting law."

How to Document Violations

If you believe the board has violated open meeting requirements, documentation is everything. Start collecting evidence before you make any formal complaint.

  • Request all meeting minutes for the past two years. Gaps, vague entries, or a pattern of "the board convened in executive session and took the following action" without any open session record are red flags.
  • Keep copies of all notices — or note in writing when no notice was provided. Date-stamp photos of posted notices (or their absence).
  • Send a written records request for agendas, notices, and minutes. If the board refuses, that refusal is itself a separate violation you can document.
  • Note who attended meetings you were excluded from and what decisions were announced afterward.
  • Follow up in email rather than phone. Every communication with the board during an active dispute should be in writing.

Once you have a documented pattern, you have options: a formal written demand to the board for compliance, a complaint to your state's HOA regulatory agency (available in some states), mediation, or consultation with an HOA attorney about injunctive relief.

What to Do If the Board Holds Secret Meetings

A "secret meeting" — a gathering where board members discuss association business without proper notice or member access — is often called a "walking quorum" or an informal gathering. These are prohibited in states with strict open meeting laws.

If you discover the board has been making decisions outside of properly noticed meetings, take the following steps:

  1. Document your evidence: emails showing decisions were pre-made, witnesses who overheard discussions, announcements made without a corresponding open meeting.
  2. Send a written demand to the board requesting that all decisions made outside of properly noticed open meetings be rescinded or ratified in a properly noticed open session.
  3. File a complaint with any applicable state regulatory body. Florida, Nevada, and California, for example, have state agencies with jurisdiction over certain HOA violations.
  4. Consult an attorney about whether the actions taken at improper meetings are legally void and challengeable in court.
Remember: This guide is educational. HOA laws vary significantly by state, and the specifics of your situation matter. Consult a licensed attorney before taking formal legal action.
