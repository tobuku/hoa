The Good Faith Clause: When a Board Crosses the Line

Every HOA board owes its members a duty of good faith and fair dealing. When boards act in their own interest — or out of personal animosity — that duty is violated. Here's how to recognize it and what to do about it.

What Good Faith and Fair Dealing Means in HOA Law

When you purchase a home in an HOA community, you enter into a legal relationship — not just with your neighbors, but with the association itself. That relationship is governed by your CC&Rs, bylaws, and state law. But underlying all of those documents is a foundational legal principle that courts have repeatedly applied to HOA governance: the duty of good faith and fair dealing.

Good faith means the board must act honestly and with sincere regard for the interests of the association and its members as a whole — not just the interests of the current board members, their allies, or themselves. Fair dealing means the board must apply rules, enforce covenants, and make decisions consistently and without improper favoritism or bias.

This duty is implied in law even when it isn't spelled out in your governing documents. In most states, courts treat HOA boards similarly to corporate directors: they hold a position of trust, and that position comes with legal obligations that members can enforce.

The Business Judgment Rule — What It Protects and What It Doesn't

You will likely encounter the "Business Judgment Rule" if you ever challenge a board decision in court. This legal doctrine provides boards with significant protection from second-guessing by courts and individual members. The idea is that boards are made up of volunteers making complex decisions under uncertainty, and courts should not substitute their own judgment for the board's on matters of business policy.

Under the Business Judgment Rule, a court will generally uphold a board's decision if:

  • The board was acting within its authority under the governing documents and state law
  • The board made an informed decision (gathered relevant information before deciding)
  • The board had a rational basis for its decision
  • The board acted in good faith, without self-interest or improper motive

The critical limitation boards often don't advertise:

The Business Judgment Rule only applies when the board was acting in good faith. It is not a blanket immunity from accountability. If a member can demonstrate that the board acted in bad faith — with personal animosity, self-interest, or discriminatory intent — the Business Judgment Rule does not protect them, and the decision can be challenged and potentially reversed by a court.

"A board cannot cloak a decision motivated by personal vendetta in the Business Judgment Rule. The protection only attaches when the board is genuinely trying to serve the association."

What "Bad Faith" Looks Like in Practice

Bad faith is not simply making a bad decision. Boards make bad decisions all the time, and they are largely protected from liability for those mistakes under the Business Judgment Rule. Bad faith requires something more — evidence that the board was not acting honestly or in the genuine interest of the community. The following are the most common forms.

Selective Enforcement

Selective enforcement occurs when the board enforces rules against some members but not others with no rational basis for the distinction. Classic examples include: citing one homeowner for a fence that violates architectural guidelines while ignoring identical fences maintained by board members' friends; sending violation notices to members who have publicly criticized the board while overlooking identical violations by compliant members; or enforcing a parking rule strictly against one household while issuing no enforcement actions against the neighboring household with the same violation.

Courts have repeatedly held that selective enforcement is a violation of the duty of good faith and, in many states, a breach of the covenant of fair dealing that renders the enforcement action invalid.

Personal Vendettas

When a board member has a personal conflict with a homeowner and then uses board authority to target that homeowner with disproportionate enforcement, repeated fines, or denial of legitimate requests, that is bad faith. Evidence of personal animosity — documented in emails, texts, or witness accounts — can transform an otherwise routine enforcement action into a legally challengeable act of retaliation.

Self-Dealing and Conflicts of Interest

Self-dealing occurs when a board member participates in a board decision that personally benefits them at the association's expense. The most common examples include: awarding contracts to vendors in which the board member has a financial interest; approving expenditures that benefit the board member's property specifically; or using HOA funds or resources for personal benefit.

Most state HOA statutes and the association's own bylaws require board members to disclose conflicts of interest and recuse themselves from related votes. When they don't, the decision may be voidable.

Failure to Apply Rules Uniformly

A board that enforces rules inconsistently — with different standards for different members, or standards that change depending on who is asking — is acting in bad faith. This is true even if no individual decision appears unreasonable on its face.

Examples of Board Actions Courts Have Found to Violate Good Faith

  • Imposing fines against a member shortly after that member filed a formal complaint against a board member, with no prior history of enforcement against that member (pattern of retaliation)
  • Denying an architectural modification request to a member the board disliked while approving virtually identical requests from other members in the same month
  • Awarding a landscaping contract worth over $100,000 to the brother-in-law of the board president without competitive bidding or disclosure of the relationship
  • Invoking emergency powers to authorize expenditures without a member vote, when no genuine emergency existed, in order to avoid anticipated member opposition
  • Denying a member access to financial records the member was entitled to inspect under state law, repeatedly and without justification

How to Document Bad Faith Behavior

Courts don't accept allegations of bad faith without evidence. Building a documented record takes time but is essential before escalating a dispute.

  • Request all records related to the board's enforcement actions: violation notices, board correspondence, meeting minutes, and vote records. Most state HOA statutes give you a right to inspect these.
  • Compile a comparative timeline: If your claim is selective enforcement, document other violations in the community that were not enforced. Photographs with GPS and date stamps, combined with your own violation record, create a compelling comparison.
  • Preserve communications: Any email, letter, or text that shows a board member expressing personal hostility or prejudging an enforcement action is valuable evidence.
  • Get witness statements: Other homeowners who have observed disparate treatment are potential witnesses.
  • Note patterns, not just incidents: A single unfavorable decision may look like a mistake. A pattern of decisions all targeting the same member tells a different story.

How to Raise a Good Faith Argument in a Dispute

In an HOA dispute, you can raise a good faith violation in several forums: a formal dispute resolution or hearing process (if your HOA or state provides one), mediation, arbitration, or litigation.

At a hearing or in a demand letter, your argument follows a logical structure:

  1. State the board action you are challenging and the specific decision or enforcement that harmed you.
  2. Identify the standard: the duty of good faith and fair dealing, and the Business Judgment Rule's requirements for good faith action.
  3. Present your evidence of bad faith: the comparative examples, the timeline, the communications, the pattern.
  4. State the remedy you are seeking: rescission of the fine, reversal of the decision, an audit of enforcement records, or other appropriate relief.

The Critical Distinction: A Bad Decision vs. Bad Faith

This distinction matters enormously. A board that hires the wrong contractor, sets the budget too high, or makes a policy decision that turns out to be wrong is not necessarily acting in bad faith. Poor judgment, if made honestly and in the genuine interest of the community, is protected by the Business Judgment Rule. Members are not entitled to override every board decision they disagree with.

Bad faith requires a showing that the board was not acting in the genuine interest of the association — that personal motive, self-interest, favoritism, or hostility drove the decision. The dividing line is intent and honesty, not outcome.

Understanding this distinction will help you evaluate whether your dispute rises to the level of a legal claim worth pursuing, or whether it is better addressed through political channels — attending meetings, running for the board, or organizing other members to vote out problematic directors.

Remember: This guide is educational. HOA laws vary significantly by state, and the specifics of your situation matter. Consult a licensed attorney before taking formal legal action.
