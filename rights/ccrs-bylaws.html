CC&Rs and Bylaws: The Contracts That Govern Your Home

Your HOA's governing documents are legally binding contracts — and you have a right to every one of them. Understanding the document hierarchy is the foundation of any successful HOA dispute.

What Are CC&Rs?

CC&Rs stands for Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions. They are the primary governing document of your homeowners association — a legally recorded document that runs with the land, meaning it binds not just current owners but all future owners of property within the community. When you purchased your home in an HOA, you agreed (whether you read them or not) to abide by the CC&Rs.

CC&Rs typically address the foundational rules of the community: what you can and cannot build, modify, or do on your property; restrictions on use (no running a business, no short-term rentals in some communities); maintenance obligations; and the association's authority to levy assessments and impose liens for nonpayment.

Because CC&Rs are recorded with the county, they are public documents. But they are also contracts — and like any contract, they must be followed by both parties. The HOA is bound by them just as much as you are. When the board enforces a provision against you that doesn't exist in the CC&Rs, or enforces one differently from how it's written, they may be in breach of the contract.

What Are Bylaws and How Do They Differ?

Bylaws govern how the HOA as an organization operates. Where CC&Rs describe the rules for living in the community, bylaws describe the rules for running the association itself: how the board is elected, how many directors serve and for how long, how meetings are noticed and conducted, what a quorum requires, and how officers are selected.

Bylaws typically do not run with the land the way CC&Rs do, though they are binding on members through the association's corporate structure. They are the HOA's internal operating manual — and most procedural disputes (improper elections, invalid votes, notice failures) are evaluated against them.

The key practical difference: if the board does something that violates the CC&Rs, it may be an improper exercise of authority. If it does something that violates the bylaws, it may be a procedural defect that makes the action legally invalid regardless of its substance.

The Hierarchy: Which Document Controls?

When governing documents conflict, a legal hierarchy determines which one prevails. Understanding this hierarchy is essential when a board claims authority it may not have.

The HOA Document Hierarchy (highest to lowest authority):
  1. Federal Law — Fair Housing Act, ADA, and other federal statutes override everything
  2. State Law — Your state's HOA or community association statutes control where they apply
  3. CC&Rs — The primary recorded governing document of the association
  4. Bylaws — The operating rules of the association's governance
  5. Rules and Regulations — Board-adopted policies, the lowest tier and easiest to change

This hierarchy has practical consequences. If a rule or regulation conflicts with the bylaws, the bylaws win. If the bylaws conflict with the CC&Rs, the CC&Rs win. If the CC&Rs conflict with state law, state law wins. A board cannot enforce a rule that contradicts a higher-authority document, even if the board adopted it by unanimous vote.

Many homeowners are fined for violating "rules" the board adopted unilaterally — without realizing that those rules may conflict with the CC&Rs and are therefore unenforceable. Always trace a rule back to its source before paying a fine for violating it.

How to Get Copies of Your Governing Documents

Getting copies of your CC&Rs, bylaws, and rules is your right — and in most states, it's a statutory right the HOA cannot withhold.

  • Your county recorder or clerk's office: CC&Rs are recorded land documents. They are public record and available through your county assessor or recorder, often online and free of charge.
  • Request from the HOA: Most state HOA statutes require the association to provide copies of governing documents to members upon request, sometimes within a specific number of days and for a reasonable copying fee. In California, Florida, and Nevada, for example, these are legally enforceable rights.
  • Your closing documents: If you recently purchased, your title company provided copies at closing. Check your escrow files.
  • HOA management company: If the association uses a professional management company, they are typically required to maintain and provide copies of governing documents.

If the board refuses or delays your records request without legal justification, that refusal is itself a potential statutory violation in most states. Document your request in writing (email creates a timestamp) and follow up in writing if you receive no response within the required timeframe.

How Amendments Work — Vote Thresholds and Notice Requirements

Governing documents can be changed, but not easily — and deliberately so. The amendment process exists to prevent a sitting board from unilaterally rewriting the rules that govern your home.

Amending CC&Rs:

CC&R amendments typically require a supermajority vote of all members — commonly 67% or 75% of the entire membership, not just those who attend a meeting. The process usually requires: written notice to all members of the proposed amendment at least 10–30 days in advance; a member vote by ballot or at a meeting; and recording the amendment with the county once passed. Some states have recently reduced thresholds to address the difficulty of reaching supermajority approval, but the requirements remain substantial.

Amending Bylaws:

Bylaws typically require a member vote as well, though the threshold may be lower than for CC&Rs (often a simple majority of a quorum, or a majority of the total membership). Proper notice of the proposed amendment is required. Some bylaws permit the board to make minor bylaw amendments without a membership vote — but this authority, if it exists, is limited and must be expressly granted in the document itself.

Adopting or Changing Rules and Regulations:

Rules and regulations are the lowest tier and can generally be adopted by a board vote alone. However, most states require advance notice to members of proposed rule changes, a comment period, and the rule cannot conflict with higher-authority documents. In California, for example, a 30-day member comment period is required before most rule changes take effect.

Common Ways Boards Violate Their Own CC&Rs

  • Enforcing "rules" that were never properly adopted or that conflict with the CC&Rs
  • Imposing fines in amounts or under procedures not authorized by the governing documents
  • Spending reserve funds for operational expenses without the member vote or board authorization required in the CC&Rs
  • Approving architectural modifications that are prohibited by the CC&Rs for favored members while denying identical requests from others
  • Amending rules without providing required member notice
  • Entering into contracts that exceed the board's authority under the CC&Rs or bylaws (e.g., a multi-year contract requiring member approval)
  • Failing to maintain common areas to the standard required by the CC&Rs

What to Do When the Board Enforces Selectively

Selective enforcement — applying the CC&Rs against some members but not others — is one of the most common and most documentable forms of HOA abuse. Courts have repeatedly ruled that a board that selectively enforces its CC&Rs may lose the right to enforce them at all against the targeted member, under the legal doctrine of waiver or estoppel.

To build a selective enforcement defense, document other violations of the same rule in your community that have not been enforced. Photographs of neighboring properties showing the same condition you were cited for, combined with your violation record and any records requests you've made, form the foundation of a selective enforcement claim.

How to Challenge an Improperly Passed Amendment

If the board announces a rule or amendment that did not follow proper procedure, you can challenge it. The steps depend on what went wrong:

  1. Identify the procedural defect: Was proper notice given? Was the vote threshold met? Was the amendment recorded (for CC&Rs)?
  2. Request documentation: Ask for the vote tally, the notice that was sent, and the meeting minutes from when the amendment was passed.
  3. Send a written objection to the board stating that the amendment was not properly adopted and is therefore invalid, citing the specific procedural requirements that were not met.
  4. Raise it at the next member meeting as a point of order or agenda item.
  5. Consult an attorney about seeking a declaratory judgment from a court that the amendment is void if the board refuses to correct the defect.

Tips for Reading Your CC&Rs Strategically

CC&Rs are dense legal documents, but you don't need to read every word. When you're in a dispute, read strategically:

  • Search for the specific rule being enforced against you. Read the exact language — not the board's summary of it.
  • Find the enforcement and fine provisions. What process is required before a fine can be imposed? Is a hearing required? Is written notice required? How many days notice?
  • Find the amendment procedures. This tells you whether a new rule was validly adopted.
  • Look for any definitions section. HOA documents often define terms specifically, and those definitions control how rules are interpreted.
  • Note what the board is required to do — not just what members must do. CC&Rs impose obligations on the association as well.
Remember: This guide is educational. HOA laws vary significantly by state, and the specifics of your situation matter. Consult a licensed attorney before taking formal legal action.
