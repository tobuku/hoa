Robert's Rules for HOA Members

Robert's Rules of Order is not just meeting etiquette. In the hands of an informed member, it's a legal toolkit for protecting your community from bad governance. Here's how to use it.

What Robert's Rules of Order Is and Why HOAs Use It

Robert's Rules of Order is a parliamentary procedure manual first published in 1876 by U.S. Army Brigadier General Henry Martyn Robert. It provides a standardized framework for conducting meetings — how to make decisions, debate issues, and ensure that minority voices are heard while majority rule is preserved.

Most HOA bylaws designate Robert's Rules of Order (or Robert's Rules of Order Newly Revised, the current edition) as the parliamentary authority governing meetings not otherwise addressed in the governing documents. This means that when your HOA's bylaws and rules are silent on a procedural question, Robert's Rules fill the gap — and they are binding.

This matters enormously for members. A board that doesn't follow Robert's Rules when they are the designated parliamentary authority is conducting meetings improperly. Decisions made at improperly conducted meetings may be challengeable. And members who know Robert's Rules can use them affirmatively — to delay bad decisions, force debate, demand votes, and protect the rights of themselves and other members.

Check your bylaws first: Before invoking Robert's Rules at a meeting, confirm that your HOA's bylaws designate it as the parliamentary authority. Most do — but some HOAs have adopted simplified meeting rules that supersede Robert's Rules.

The Six Most Powerful Motions an HOA Member Can Make

Understanding these six motions gives you procedural tools most board members assume their members don't know. Use them calmly, correctly, and strategically.

1. Motion to Table (Postpone Indefinitely or to a Time Certain)

A motion to table asks the assembly to set aside a pending matter — either indefinitely or until a specific future date. In HOA meetings, this is useful when the board is rushing through an important decision without adequate information or member input.

How to use it: When a motion is on the floor, say: "Madam Chair, I move to postpone this matter to [date/next meeting] to allow for additional member input." The motion requires a second, is not debatable in its basic form, and requires a majority vote to pass. If it passes, the matter cannot be taken up again until the specified time.

A motion to postpone indefinitely effectively kills the main motion for that meeting — a powerful tool when you need time to organize opposition.

2. Point of Order

A Point of Order is your procedural objection. It does not require a second and can be raised immediately when a rule is being violated. You do not wait to be recognized by the chair — you simply stand and say "Point of Order."

When to use it: When the chair attempts to proceed without a quorum, when a vote is conducted improperly, when a member is denied the right to speak, when an agenda item is skipped without consent of the assembly, or when any other rule is being violated in real time. The chair must rule on your point immediately. If you disagree with the ruling, you can appeal the decision of the chair (see below).

3. Motion to Amend

When a motion is on the floor, you can propose changes to it before it is voted on. A motion to amend lets you modify the wording, add conditions, or strike portions of what is being proposed.

How to use it: "I move to amend the motion by [inserting / striking / substituting] the following language: [text]." Requires a second and a majority vote to adopt the amendment. The amended motion then becomes the version voted on. This is particularly useful when you support the general idea of a board proposal but not the specific terms.

4. Call the Question (Previous Question)

This motion forces an immediate vote on the matter being debated — cutting off further discussion. It requires a two-thirds vote to pass.

When it's useful for members: When the board is using prolonged discussion to delay a vote they know they might lose, calling the question forces the matter to a vote. Conversely, be aware that the board can use this motion against you to shut down member debate — which is why you need to know it too.

5. Motion to Rescind

A motion to rescind asks the assembly to repeal or cancel a prior action the board has taken. This is one of the most powerful tools available to members — it can undo a board decision made at a previous meeting.

How to use it: "I move to rescind the action taken at the [date] meeting regarding [matter]." In most cases, rescission requires a two-thirds vote or a majority of the entire membership. If the board has taken an action without proper authority, or if circumstances have materially changed since the original decision, rescission is the parliamentary path to reversal. Note: actions that have already been carried out (contracts signed, money spent) cannot typically be rescinded retroactively, but the board can be directed to take corrective action.

6. Division of the Assembly

When you believe a voice vote or show-of-hands was counted incorrectly, call for a Division of the Assembly. This forces a retake of the vote by a more verifiable method — typically a standing count or written ballot.

How to use it: Immediately after a vote is announced, say "Division!" You don't need recognition or a second. The chair must order a recount by a more definitive method. This motion is essential when you believe the board is misrepresenting vote tallies.

How to Challenge a Board Vote That Was Conducted Improperly

If a vote was taken under improper conditions — without quorum, without proper notice of the agenda item, by an invalid method, or in violation of your governing documents — you have several options:

  1. Raise a Point of Order immediately if you catch the problem in real time. Have the chair rule on the procedural defect.
  2. Appeal the Decision of the Chair if the chair rules against your Point of Order. Say "I appeal the decision of the chair." The appeal requires a second and is decided by majority vote of the assembly. The board does not get to vote on this — if members are present, they vote on whether to sustain or reverse the chair's ruling.
  3. Request that the defect be noted in the minutes. After the meeting, review the minutes to ensure the procedural objection is recorded. If it is not, submit a written correction before the minutes are approved at the next meeting.
  4. Challenge the action formally in writing to the board after the meeting, stating that the vote was procedurally invalid and requesting that the action be rescinded or re-voted at a properly conducted meeting.

How to Get an Item Added to the Agenda

In most HOA meetings, the agenda is set by the board. However, members typically have several avenues for adding items:

  • Submit a written request before the meeting. Many HOA bylaws and state statutes allow members to submit agenda items in writing within a specified period before the meeting. California, for example, allows members to submit agenda items for the next regular board meeting.
  • Use the member open forum. Most HOA meetings include a member comment or open forum period. While you cannot typically force action at this point, you can formally request that an item be placed on the next agenda and state your reasons.
  • Request a special meeting. If you can gather enough member signatures (check your bylaws for the required number — often 5–10% of membership), you can petition for a special membership meeting to address a specific issue.

Your Right to Speak at Meetings

Open meeting laws and Robert's Rules together generally entitle you to speak during the member open forum — but not to unlimited debate on every board agenda item. Know the structure:

  • You have a right to attend and observe all open session business
  • During member open forum, you can raise any topic relevant to the association — the board does not have to respond, but your comments are on the record
  • On agenda items, many HOA bylaws and some state statutes give members a right to comment before a vote is taken. California's Davis-Stirling Act, for example, requires a reasonable opportunity for members to comment on each agenda item.
  • Time limits on member comments (typically 3–5 minutes per person) are generally valid as long as they are applied uniformly

What Quorum Means and How to Use It

Quorum is the minimum number of members or directors who must be present for official business to be conducted. Without quorum, no valid votes can be taken.

For board meetings, quorum is typically a majority of the board (e.g., 3 of 5 directors). For membership meetings (annual elections, amendment votes), quorum is usually defined in the bylaws as a percentage of the total membership.

Strategically, quorum matters in two ways:

  • As a shield: If a board meeting begins without quorum, you can raise a Point of Order stating that quorum has not been established. No business conducted without quorum is valid.
  • As a lever at membership meetings: If a controversial vote is scheduled and you can persuade enough members to stay away, the meeting may fail to achieve quorum — preventing the vote from happening until a new meeting is called with potentially different attendance. This is a political tactic, not a legal one, but it is entirely within the rules.

Step-by-Step: How to Challenge a Board Action at a Meeting

  1. Prepare before you arrive. Know the specific rule being violated and the specific provision of Robert's Rules or your governing documents that applies. Write it down.
  2. Wait for the right moment. Raise procedural objections at the moment the violation occurs — not retroactively during general discussion.
  3. Stand and state your motion or point clearly and calmly. "Point of Order. The motion on the floor was voted on before members were given an opportunity to comment, in violation of Section [X] of our bylaws." Do not shout. Do not argue. State the issue and sit down.
  4. Listen to the chair's ruling. If you disagree, immediately appeal: "I appeal the decision of the chair." Move quickly — the right to appeal expires if you delay.
  5. Document everything after the meeting. Review the draft minutes and submit corrections if your objection was omitted or mischaracterized. All corrections should be in writing.
  6. Follow up in writing. If the procedural defect was significant, send the board a letter within a few days summarizing what occurred, what rule was violated, and what action you expect the board to take to correct it.
Remember: This guide is educational. HOA laws vary significantly by state, and the specifics of your situation matter. Consult a licensed attorney before taking formal legal action.
