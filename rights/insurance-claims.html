Insurance Claims & HOA Disputes: Who Pays for What

When disaster strikes a property in an HOA community, the question of who covers what can determine whether a family recovers or loses everything. This is the guide we wish every HOA member had before they needed it.

This is one of the most consequential gaps in HOA member knowledge. The split between the HOA's master policy and your individual policy is often not explained clearly at purchase — and discovering the gap after a catastrophic loss can be devastating. Read this entire guide before you need it.

The HOA Master Policy vs. Your Individual Homeowner's Policy

In an HOA community, insurance responsibility is divided — and that division is rarely as intuitive as members expect. There are fundamentally two layers of insurance coverage:

The HOA's master policy is purchased by the association and covers the association's shared interests: common areas (pools, clubhouses, hallways, exterior structures, roofs in many cases), the physical structure of the building in condo communities, and the association's liability for injuries occurring in common areas. The HOA pays the premiums from association dues, and it is the association — not individual members — that holds the policy.

Your individual homeowner's policy (often called an HO-6 policy for condo units, or HO-3 for single-family homes) covers your personal property, your unit's interior, and your personal liability. What falls within the scope of your individual policy versus the HOA's master policy depends critically on what type of master policy the HOA carries.

Walls-In vs. Bare Walls Coverage — The Critical Distinction

This single distinction determines coverage for the most expensive part of a condo loss: the unit itself.

Bare Walls Coverage (also called "studs-out")

Under a bare walls policy, the HOA's master policy covers only the structural elements of the building: the framing, exterior walls, roofing, and common plumbing and electrical systems that run through walls. Everything inside the unit — flooring, cabinetry, countertops, fixtures, drywall, appliances, and any improvements you've made — is your responsibility to insure under your own policy.

If your HOA carries bare walls coverage, your individual HO-6 policy needs to be robust enough to cover complete unit interior reconstruction in a total loss.

Walls-In Coverage (also called "all-in" or "all-inclusive")

Under a walls-in policy, the HOA's master policy covers not just the structure but also the standard fixtures and finishes inside your unit as originally constructed — flooring, cabinetry, built-in appliances, and similar features. Your individual policy then needs to cover only your personal property and any upgrades you've made above the original standard.

Under a walls-in policy, a member's individual coverage gap is significantly smaller — but many members wrongly assume their association has walls-in coverage when it has bare walls, or vice versa.

"The most common catastrophic coverage gap we see is a condo owner with a bare-walls master policy and an HO-6 policy with building coverage limits far too low to rebuild their unit. They assumed the HOA had them covered. It didn't."

What you must do right now:

  1. Request a copy of the HOA's current master insurance policy from the board or management company. This is a document you are entitled to see.
  2. Determine whether it is a bare walls, walls-in, or somewhere-in-between policy. Look for the terms "all-in," "bare walls," "building coverage," or "unit owner improvements."
  3. Share that information with your personal insurance agent and ask whether your HO-6 coverage is sized appropriately to fill the gap.

What the HOA's Insurance Typically Covers vs. What Yours Covers

HOA master policy typically covers:

  • Common areas: hallways, lobbies, pools, recreational facilities, parking structures
  • Exterior structures: roofing, siding, exterior doors and windows (in most policies)
  • Common area liability: injuries to guests or residents occurring in common areas
  • Directors and Officers (D&O) liability: coverage for board members against certain claims arising from governance decisions
  • In walls-in policies: standard unit fixtures and finishes as originally constructed

Your individual policy typically covers:

  • Personal property: furniture, electronics, clothing, and belongings
  • Unit interior (under bare walls HOA policies): flooring, cabinetry, fixtures, appliances
  • Upgrades and improvements you've made to the unit above original construction standards
  • Loss assessment coverage: when the HOA's loss exceeds its master policy limits and the shortfall is assessed to members
  • Personal liability for incidents occurring within your unit
  • Additional living expenses if you're displaced while your unit is repaired
Loss Assessment Coverage: Many HOA members don't realize that if a major disaster causes damage exceeding the master policy's limits, the HOA can special-assess members to cover the shortfall. Your individual HO-6 policy can include "loss assessment coverage" that protects you from this risk. Ask your insurance agent if yours does.

What Happens When the HOA Denies or Delays Supporting Your Claim

One of the most devastating scenarios an HOA member can face is filing a claim after a disaster — and finding the board uncooperative, unresponsive, or actively obstructive. This happens more than it should, for a range of reasons: boards that don't want to file a claim for fear of premium increases, boards that dispute whether the damage falls within the master policy, or boards that are simply negligent or disorganized.

When the board fails to act on a claim that appears to be covered by the master policy, you have legal rights:

  • The board has a fiduciary duty to maintain insurance coverage adequate to protect the association and its members, and to pursue valid claims under that coverage.
  • Most state HOA statutes require associations to carry specified types and amounts of insurance, and to use those proceeds for the intended purpose of repairing covered damage.
  • In many states, unreasonable delay or denial of a valid insurance claim by the insurer — or failure to pursue a claim by the board — may give you a legal claim for bad faith or breach of fiduciary duty.

How to Document Damage for an HOA-Related Insurance Dispute

Documentation is the foundation of every successful insurance claim. When the loss involves shared property or a question of HOA vs. individual responsibility, thorough documentation becomes even more important.

  • Photograph and video everything immediately — before any cleanup or repair, document every affected area thoroughly with timestamps.
  • Obtain independent repair estimates from licensed contractors. Do not rely solely on the board's or insurer's estimate of damage scope.
  • Request the insurance adjuster's report once the HOA files a claim. As a member, you may be entitled to this information, particularly if the claim affects your unit.
  • Document the timeline of your communications with the board: date sent, method, content, and response received.
  • Request a copy of the master policy so you know exactly what is covered and what the claims process requires.
  • Hire a public adjuster if the claim is substantial. Public adjusters work for you — not the insurance company — and are skilled at maximizing legitimate claim recovery.

Steps When the HOA Is Unresponsive: Escalation Path

If the board is failing to respond to a legitimate insurance claim situation, escalate through the following steps in order:

  1. Written demand to the board — Send a certified letter specifying the damage, the coverage you believe applies under the master policy, and a deadline for the board to take action. This creates a documented record that you gave the board opportunity to act.
  2. Review the master policy directly — Request a copy of the full policy, read the claims process section, and determine whether you can contact the insurer directly as an additional insured (you may be listed as one).
  3. File a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance — If the HOA's insurer is wrongfully denying or delaying a valid claim, the state insurance commissioner has regulatory authority over insurers and can investigate bad faith claims handling.
  4. File a complaint with your state's HOA regulatory body — Several states have agencies with jurisdiction over HOA governance failures, including failures related to insurance obligations.
  5. Consult an HOA attorney or a bad faith insurance attorney — If the board's failure to act has caused you quantifiable loss, you may have a claim against the board for breach of fiduciary duty, or against the insurer for bad faith.

Natural Disaster Considerations: Fire, Flood, and Hurricane

Natural disasters complicate HOA insurance disputes because the stakes are higher, multiple units may be affected simultaneously, and the HOA's resources may be strained.

Fire: In a multi-unit building fire, the HOA's master policy is typically the primary source of coverage for structural damage. Members should verify whether their individual policies include loss of use coverage for displacement, and whether the master policy covers debris removal and code compliance upgrades (rebuilding to current code can cost significantly more than restoring to pre-loss condition).

Flood: Standard HOA master policies — like standard homeowner policies — typically do not cover flood damage. Flood coverage requires a separate flood insurance policy through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or a private insurer. If your HOA community is in a flood zone, determine whether the HOA carries a flood policy covering common areas and structures, and whether you need individual flood coverage for your unit and contents.

Hurricane/Wind: In hurricane-prone states (Florida, Texas, Louisiana, the Carolinas), wind and hurricane damage coverage is sometimes excluded from standard master policies and requires a separate windstorm policy. Verify what your HOA's master policy covers and what it excludes before storm season.

The HOA's Duty to Maintain Common Areas vs. Your Property

The HOA has a legal duty to maintain common areas — and when negligent maintenance of common areas causes damage to your unit or property, the HOA (and thus the master policy) may be liable. For example: a leaking roof the HOA has failed to repair that causes water damage to your unit; a broken common area pipe that floods several units; or a failing retaining wall that damages adjacent property. Document the HOA's failure to maintain the common area that caused your loss, report it in writing, and preserve that record for any subsequent insurance or legal dispute.

What to Do If the Board Steered the Insurance Contract to Connected Parties

Insurance contracts represent some of the largest expenditures in an HOA budget. When a board awards an insurance contract to a company in which a board member has a financial interest — or to a broker who provides personal benefits to a board member — that is self-dealing, a violation of the duty of good faith, and potentially illegal under state law.

Signs of insurance steering include: the board not taking competitive bids on coverage renewal; a significant and unexplained increase in premiums without corresponding increase in coverage; the same insurance broker being used for many years without re-evaluation; or a board member disclosing (or failing to disclose) a relationship with the insurance broker.

Request copies of the bid process used for the current insurance contract. Ask for documentation of competitive bids. If none exist, or if the board cannot explain the selection process, raise the matter formally at a board meeting and demand that the next renewal go through a documented competitive process.

Remember: This guide is educational. HOA laws and insurance regulations vary significantly by state, and the specifics of your situation matter. Consult a licensed attorney and/or a public adjuster for your specific situation.
