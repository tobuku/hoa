About This Site

Why This Site Exists

One homeowner's experience with HOA injustice became the foundation for a national resource — built so no one else has to learn the hard way.

The Story Behind This Site

I am a homeowner in Hawaii. My family lost our home and nearly all of our possessions in a brush fire.

What followed was worse than the fire itself.

The community association I belonged to — the one that was supposed to look out for the community's interests — refused to cooperate with our insurance claim. The board used procedural delays, withheld key information, and exploited our vulnerability at the worst possible moment in our lives. When we needed help most, the system we had paid into for years turned against us.

I didn't know my rights. And the board counted on that.

What happened next changed my life. I spent months learning HOA law — not from a law school, but from sheer necessity. I learned about open meeting requirements (the Sunshine Clause). I learned about the Good Faith and Fair Dealing obligations that boards are supposed to uphold. I learned Robert's Rules of Order, how to read CC&Rs and bylaws, how insurance claims work in HOA contexts, how board elections can be manipulated, how contracts get quietly steered to connected parties, and how to formally document everything.

I learned how to file for mediation. We went through that process. We prevailed.

But it shouldn't have taken that.

Taking It to the Legislature

After our experience, I connected with other homeowners who had faced similar situations. What we found was a pattern: HOA boards operating with minimal oversight, management companies with financial incentives to serve boards rather than members, and homeowners left with no practical recourse short of expensive litigation.

We worked to introduce legislation in Hawaii that would have created independent state oversight of HOA management companies — a system of accountability that would have given homeowners somewhere to turn besides a courtroom.

The bill failed. It was lobbied out of existence by the very management companies it would have regulated. They had more resources, more connections, and more time than a group of homeowners who were trying to rebuild their lives.

That experience was clarifying. Industry lobbying will always outspend grassroots homeowner advocacy in legislative chambers. The only sustainable solution is widespread homeowner education. When enough members know their rights, boards cannot exploit ignorance as a strategy. The power dynamic shifts.

Why This Site is Free

This site doesn't require a login. It doesn't charge a membership fee. It doesn't collect your personal information beyond what you voluntarily submit in a contact form. It doesn't have investors to answer to.

It exists because I needed something like this and it didn't exist. The resources that were available were either written by HOA management companies (with obvious conflicts of interest), written by attorneys marketing their services, or so buried in legal language that a regular homeowner couldn't use them.

Every guide on this site is written in plain English for the homeowner who is scared, confused, and feeling powerless. Because that's who I was. And that's who deserves real information.

What This Site Is — and Isn't

This site is not a law firm. Nothing here is legal advice, and no attorney-client relationship is created by using this site. HOA laws vary significantly by state. For your specific situation, please consult a licensed attorney in your state. Our attorney directory can help you find one.

What this site is: a collection of educational resources built from real experience, real research, and a genuine desire to help the 74 million Americans living in HOAs understand the rights they already have.

Help Us Grow

How You Can Help

This site grows through community. Here's how you can contribute:

  • Share this site with anyone you know who is dealing with an HOA dispute. The more people who know it exists, the more effective it becomes.
  • Submit corrections — HOA laws change. If you spot outdated information, please contact us.
  • Attorneys: If you practice HOA law on behalf of homeowners (not just boards), submit your listing to the directory — it's free.
  • Leave a review of your HOA management company to help other homeowners make informed decisions.
  • Share your experience. If your state's page doesn't exist yet, and you have knowledge of your state's HOA laws, reach out. We want to build every state page with input from homeowners who know it firsthand.
