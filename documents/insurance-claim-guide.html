Insurance Claims When Your HOA Is Involved

When disaster strikes an HOA property, navigating two insurance systems at once is overwhelming. This guide explains what you're entitled to, who is responsible for what, and what to do when the HOA won't cooperate.

If you have just experienced a loss: File claims with both your individual insurer AND the HOA's insurer immediately — do not wait for the HOA board to act on your behalf. Time limits apply. Document everything with photos and video before any cleanup begins.

Understanding the Two Insurance Systems

Every HOA property involves at least two separate insurance policies, and understanding the difference is critical before a loss occurs:

The HOA's Master Policy

The HOA carries a master policy that covers the common areas and, depending on the policy type, may cover portions of the individual units or homes. There are two main types:

"Bare Walls In" (All-In) Coverage

The HOA's policy covers everything inside the walls of each unit — floors, cabinets, fixtures, appliances — down to the bare walls and subfloor. Your individual policy (HO-6) is primarily for personal property and liability. This type is more common in older condo associations.

"Walls In" (Single Entity) Coverage — More Common

The HOA's policy covers only the building's structure and common elements. Everything inside your unit — including fixtures, cabinets, flooring, built-ins — is your responsibility under your individual HO-6 policy. This is the more common structure today.

Critical action item: Request a copy of the HOA's master policy declarations page and confirm which type of coverage applies to your community. You have the right to this document as an HOA member. Request it in writing.

Your Individual HO-6 Policy

If you own a condo or a home in a planned community, you should carry an HO-6 (condo) or HO-3 (single family) policy. This policy covers your personal property, interior improvements (in a walls-in HOA), and personal liability. It may also carry "loss assessment coverage" — which pays your share of special assessments the HOA levies to cover a loss not fully covered by the master policy.

Immediate Steps After a Loss

  1. Photograph and video everything immediately — before any cleanup, removal, or remediation. This documentation is your most important evidence.
  2. Notify your individual insurer the same day. Do not wait for the HOA to act first.
  3. Send written notice to the HOA board by certified mail — formally notifying them of the loss and requesting that they notify their insurer and provide you a copy of the master policy.
  4. Request the HOA's insurance carrier name, policy number, and claims contact information. You have the right to this information as an HOA member.
  5. Do not sign any documents or accept any payments from either insurer until you fully understand what coverage is being applied and what is being waived.

When the HOA Delays or Is Unresponsive

HOA boards sometimes delay notifying their insurer, fail to cooperate with your claim, or even actively work against your interests in a claim. If this happens:

Step 1: Formal Written Demand

Send a written demand to the board (certified mail) requiring them to: (1) notify the master insurer, (2) provide the insurer's contact information, and (3) cooperate with your claim. Set a 10-day deadline. See the demand letter template.

Step 2: Contact the HOA's Insurer Directly

In many cases, you can contact the HOA's insurance company directly as an affected party. They have their own obligation to investigate claims. The HOA board does not have the authority to instruct their insurer to ignore a legitimate claim.

Step 3: State Insurance Commissioner Complaint

If either the HOA's insurer or your own insurer is acting in bad faith — delaying unreasonably, denying without adequate explanation, or misrepresenting coverage — file a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance. Insurers take these complaints seriously; state regulators have enforcement authority.

Step 4: Public Adjuster

A licensed public adjuster works on your behalf (not the insurer's) to document the full scope of your loss and negotiate a fair settlement. They typically work on contingency (a percentage of the claim). For large losses involving HOA complications, a public adjuster can be worth their fee.

Step 5: HOA Attorney

If the board is actively obstructing your insurance claim — refusing to notify their insurer, denying you access to policy documents, misrepresenting coverage to you — this may constitute a breach of their fiduciary duty. An HOA attorney can advise you on whether you have grounds for legal action and whether your state's fee-shifting statutes apply.

Natural Disaster Considerations

Wildfire / Brush Fire

In wildfire-affected communities, the HOA's master policy may be the first line of coverage for structural damage to common elements. However, the HOA board controls when and how claims are filed. If the board delays filing or accepts an inadequate settlement from the master insurer, members may be left with a large special assessment to cover the gap. Document your losses independently and demand copies of all claim filings and settlements.

Hurricane / Tropical Storm

Hurricane coverage often has separate, higher deductibles (percentage-based rather than flat amounts). In Florida and other hurricane-prone states, the HOA's master policy hurricane deductible may be significant — and if the total loss exceeds the policy coverage, the HOA can levy a special assessment on all members for the remainder. Know your association's coverage limits and reserve fund balance before a storm season.

Red Flags: Signs the HOA Is Mishandling Your Claim

  • The board refuses to provide the master policy documents
  • The board delays notifying the master insurer without explanation
  • The board accepts a settlement from the master insurer without involving affected members in the process
  • The board levies a special assessment for "uninsured losses" without disclosing how the claim was handled
  • The board's insurance carrier happens to be an agency connected to a board member or their family
  • The insurance contract is renewed without competitive bids or board vote

Bottom Line: You do not have to rely solely on the HOA board to protect your interests in an insurance claim. You have the right to information, the right to contact insurers directly, and the right to legal recourse if the board fails its duty. Act quickly — insurance claims have strict time limits.
