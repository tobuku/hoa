How to Challenge an HOA Board Vote

If your board is violating meeting procedures, you have the right — and the tools — to challenge it on the spot and in writing afterward. Here's how.

Read this before your next meeting. The time to challenge a procedural violation is during the meeting, not after. Once a vote is recorded in the minutes and the board moves on, challenges become much harder. Preparation is everything.

When to Challenge a Meeting or Vote

Not every board action you disagree with is challengeable on procedural grounds. A challenge is appropriate when:

  • The meeting was not properly noticed (insufficient advance notice, or notice did not include the agenda item being voted on)
  • Quorum was not present when the vote was taken
  • The vote threshold required by the CC&Rs or bylaws was not met
  • The board voted on a matter that requires a member vote under your governing documents or state statute
  • The chair ruled on a procedural matter incorrectly
  • Members were improperly excluded from the meeting or prevented from speaking
  • An executive session was used for matters that don't qualify for closed session

During the Meeting: How to Raise a Point of Order

A Point of Order is a formal challenge to a procedural violation under Robert's Rules. Here is how to use it:

Step-by-Step: Raising a Point of Order

  1. Stand and address the chair: "Point of Order."
  2. Wait to be recognized (the chair must recognize a Point of Order immediately — they cannot ignore it or delay it).
  3. State the violation clearly and specifically: "The chair has just called a vote on the [budget amendment / special assessment / contract award], but this item was not included on the agenda provided to members. Under [Article X of our Bylaws / Robert's Rules], matters not on the agenda cannot be voted on at this meeting."
  4. The chair must rule — either agree with your point (sustaining it) or disagree (overruling it).
  5. If overruled and you believe the ruling is wrong, immediately state: "I appeal the ruling of the chair." This forces the full membership present to vote on whether the chair's ruling was correct. A majority of members present can overrule the chair.

Requesting a Vote Recount: Division of the Assembly

If you believe a voice vote or show-of-hands vote was miscount, you can demand a precise count:

"I call for Division of the Assembly."

This requires the chair to conduct a precise, countable vote (standing count, written ballot, or roll call) rather than accepting an unclear voice vote. This is an absolute right — the chair cannot refuse it.

Forcing a Member Vote on a Board Decision

If the board is voting on something that your governing documents or state statute require to be approved by the membership (not just the board), raise the Point of Order immediately. Common examples:

  • Special assessments above the statutory or CC&R threshold
  • Amendments to the CC&Rs or bylaws (always requires member vote)
  • Major capital expenditures above the board's authority limit
  • Changes to use restrictions

After the Meeting: Written Objection

If the board overruled your challenge or the meeting has already occurred and you believe a procedural violation took place, send a written objection within days — not weeks. Use the demand letter template and include:

  • The date and location of the meeting
  • The specific vote or action you are challenging
  • The specific procedural rule that was violated (cite your bylaws section and/or state statute)
  • The remedy you are requesting (rescission of the vote, a new meeting with proper notice, etc.)
  • A deadline for response

Send this via certified mail. Keep a copy. This letter becomes part of the official paper trail.

When to Escalate Beyond the Board

If the board refuses to correct the procedural violation, your options include:

  • State HOA regulatory body: File a complaint with your state's HOA oversight agency. See the state directory for the correct agency in your state.
  • Mediation/ADR: Request formal Alternative Dispute Resolution if your state requires it before litigation.
  • Member petition: Most HOAs allow members to petition for a special meeting if enough members sign. Check your bylaws for the required signature threshold (often 10–25% of members).
  • Legal action: A court can void an improperly taken board action. Consult an HOA attorney about whether the violation warrants litigation and whether fee-shifting statutes in your state could make it financially viable.
