HOA Member Rights Checklist

Know exactly what rights you have before your next board meeting or dispute. Print this page and bring it with you.

← All Documents

How to use this checklist: Review each item before a board meeting or dispute. Check items you've already confirmed. Use unchecked items as your action list. Laws vary by state — see the state directory for your specific statutes.

📋 Section 1: Your Basic Rights as an HOA Member
  • Right to governing documents: You have the right to receive copies of all CC&Rs, bylaws, and rules and regulations. The HOA must provide these upon request, often within a specified number of days.
  • Right to attend open board meetings: Most state laws require HOA board meetings to be open to all members. You cannot legally be excluded from an open meeting.
  • Right to speak at meetings: You have the right to address the board during the homeowner forum or open comment period. Check your bylaws for time limits (typically 3–5 minutes per member).
  • Right to vote: You have the right to vote in all association elections, including board elections, amendments to governing documents, and major expenditure approvals.
  • Right to inspect records: You have the right to inspect financial records, meeting minutes, contracts, and other official association records. Most states require a response within 5–10 business days.
  • Right to proper advance notice: You must receive adequate advance notice of all board meetings and member meetings. Most states require 10–30 days notice for regular meetings; check your state's requirement.
  • Right to consistent rule enforcement: The board must enforce rules consistently and without discrimination. Selective enforcement — applying rules against some members but not others — is a violation of the Good Faith clause in most states.
  • Right to dispute resolution: Most states require the HOA to offer Internal Dispute Resolution (IDR) or Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR/mediation) before litigation is pursued. The HOA typically cannot sue you without first offering this.
💰 Section 2: Financial Rights
  • Annual budget disclosure: You have the right to receive the annual budget at least 30 days before it takes effect in most states. The board generally cannot increase regular assessments above a certain percentage without a member vote.
  • Financial audit or review: Many states require HOAs above a certain size to conduct an annual audit or financial review that members can request and inspect.
  • Reserve fund information: You have the right to know the current reserve fund balance and to review the most recent reserve study. Underfunded reserves should be disclosed.
  • Receipts and invoices: You have the right to inspect receipts, invoices, and contracts for major expenditures. Request the contracts for any service the HOA has retained, especially large recurring contracts.
  • Special assessment vote: Most state statutes require a member vote before the board can impose a special assessment above a certain threshold. Check your state's statute for the threshold amount and required vote percentage.
  • Due process for fines: Before a fine is assessed, most states require that you receive written notice of the violation and an opportunity to be heard before an independent fines committee or the board. Fines assessed without this process may be unenforceable.
  • Right to payment plan: Many state statutes require HOAs to offer reasonable payment plans for delinquent assessments before pursuing collections or foreclosure.
🗳️ Section 3: Meeting & Voting Rights
  • Verify meeting notice: Confirm that meeting notices were sent within the required advance notice period and included a proper agenda. Votes taken at a meeting where proper notice was not given may be voidable.
  • Confirm quorum: Before any vote is taken, the board or meeting chair must confirm that quorum is present. Votes taken without quorum are not valid. Know your association's quorum requirement (typically in the bylaws).
  • Add agenda items: Check your bylaws for the procedure to add items to the agenda. Many associations allow members to submit agenda items a specified number of days in advance.
  • Point of Order: If you believe a meeting procedure is being violated, you may raise a "Point of Order" under Robert's Rules. State clearly: "Point of Order — the chair is [describe the procedural error]." The chair must rule on the point immediately.
  • Minutes documentation: Meeting minutes must be recorded and distributed to members within a reasonable period (often 30 days). You have the right to request corrections to inaccurate minutes.
  • Executive session limitations: Boards may go into executive (closed) session only for specific purposes: pending litigation, personnel matters, contract negotiations, and member disciplinary hearings. Secret meetings on general business matters are a violation in most states.
  • Secret ballot elections: Many states (including California) require HOA elections to be conducted by secret ballot with an independent Inspector of Elections. Ask your state's requirements if you suspect election manipulation.
⚡ Section 4: Dispute & Escalation Rights
  • Document everything in writing: Every interaction with the board or management company in a dispute should be in writing. Send emails and follow up verbal conversations with written summaries. Paper trail = leverage.
  • Certified mail: For formal requests and complaints, use certified mail with return receipt requested. This creates proof of delivery that cannot be disputed.
  • Request Internal Dispute Resolution (IDR): Most states require HOAs to offer IDR — an informal meeting to resolve disputes — before escalating. Request IDR in writing. The HOA's refusal to participate may itself be a violation.
  • State regulatory body: Most states have a government body that has some oversight of HOAs or can accept complaints. Know which agency handles HOAs in your state (see the state directory).
  • Attorney's fee shifting: Several states have statutes that require the HOA to pay your attorney fees if you win certain types of disputes. Ask an attorney whether this applies in your state before assuming you can't afford to fight.
  • Statute of limitations: HOA claims have time limits. Know the statute of limitations in your state for the type of claim you're making. Missing the deadline can bar your claim entirely.
  • Mediation before litigation: Many states require the parties to attempt mediation before filing a lawsuit. Some state agencies (like Arizona's OAH) offer low-cost administrative hearings as an alternative to court.
🚩 Section 5: Red Flags — Signs Your Board May Be Acting in Bad Faith
  • Board holds meetings without proper notice or in locations that are difficult to access
  • Board awards large contracts to members of the board, their family members, or business associates without competitive bidding
  • Rules are enforced against some members but not others — especially not against board members or their friends
  • Board refuses or delays providing records within the legally required timeframe
  • Board increases assessments or imposes special assessments without a proper member vote when one is required
  • Board amends CC&Rs or bylaws without achieving the required super-majority vote or proper notice
  • Board retaliates against members who ask questions, vote against proposals, or speak at meetings
  • Management company contract is renewed without competitive bids or board vote
  • Reserve fund is significantly below recommended levels without a clear plan for funding
  • Board refuses to answer questions about how a specific contractor was selected or what they were paid
  • Meeting minutes are consistently vague, incomplete, or not distributed within the required timeframe
  • Board uses executive session for matters that do not qualify (general business, budget discussions)

What to do if you checked multiple red flags: Document everything, request all records in writing via certified mail, and consult with an HOA attorney in your state. The more red flags, the more important it is to act quickly — HOA disputes have statutes of limitations.
