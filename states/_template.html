How to Find [STATE NAME]'s HOA Laws Right Now

While we build this page, you don't have to wait. Here's how to find your state's HOA statutes directly, for free, on the official state legislature website.

Search for the Planned Communities Act or HOA Act

Most states have a statute that specifically governs planned community homeowners associations — sometimes called the "Planned Communities Act," "Property Owners Association Act," "Homeowners Association Act," or similar. To find it:

  1. Go to your state legislature's official website. Search for "[STATE NAME] state legislature" and look for the official .gov site.
  2. Use the site's statute search function and search for: "[STATE NAME] HOA statutes", "[STATE NAME] planned communities act", or "[STATE NAME] homeowners association".
  3. Look for a chapter or title that specifically addresses property owners associations, planned community associations, or common interest developments.
  4. Download or bookmark the relevant chapters — they are typically free to view and download from official state websites.

Search for the Condominium Act (if you own a condo)

If you own a condominium unit rather than a single-family home, your association is governed by a separate statute — usually the state's Condominium Act or Uniform Condominium Act. Search your state legislature's website for "[STATE NAME] condominium act" to find the applicable law.

Use Google with the Right Search Terms

If the state legislature website is hard to navigate, try a targeted Google search:

  • "[STATE NAME] HOA statutes site:.gov" — limits results to official government sites

What to Look for in Any State's HOA Law

Regardless of which state you're in, there are six core questions every HOA member should be able to answer by reading their state's statutes. Use this as a checklist while you research [STATE NAME] law.

📋

Records Access

Does the law give you the right to inspect association records? What records are included? How many days does the association have to respond? What's the remedy if they refuse?

🏛️

Open Meetings

Are board meetings required to be open to members? How much advance notice is required? When can the board go into closed session? Are minutes required to be distributed?

🗳️

Voting Rights

What requires a full member vote vs. just a board vote? What vote threshold is needed for amendments? Can members recall board members? How are elections conducted?

⚖️

Fines Due Process

What notice must the association give before imposing a fine? Do you have the right to a hearing? Who conducts the hearing — the full board, or an independent committee? What are the maximum fines?

🏠

Foreclosure Protections

Can your HOA foreclose on your home for unpaid assessments? Is court approval (judicial foreclosure) required, or can it happen without a court order? What's the minimum debt threshold? Are there notice requirements?

📬

Dispute Resolution

Is there a state agency you can complain to, or is court your only option? Is mediation or arbitration required before filing suit? Are attorney's fees available if you win? What's the statute of limitations?

General HOA Rights That Apply in Most States

While the specifics vary significantly by state, the following rights are recognized in most state HOA statutes. These aren't guaranteed in every jurisdiction, but they are a reasonable starting point for understanding what protections you may have.

  • Right to obtain copies of your governing documents. In virtually every state, you are entitled to a copy of the Declaration, CC&Rs, Bylaws, and Rules. The association may charge for copying but cannot deny access.
  • Right to notice of board meetings. Most states require the board to post or distribute notice of regular meetings in advance, typically 48 hours to two weeks depending on state law.
  • Right to attend board meetings. The majority of states require board meetings to be open to members, with limited exceptions for executive sessions on sensitive matters.
  • Right to notice of violations and an opportunity to respond. Before a fine can be imposed, you are generally entitled to notice of the alleged violation and a chance to be heard.
  • Right to vote on major decisions. Amendments to the CC&Rs, large special assessments, and other significant matters typically require a vote of the full membership, not just the board.
  • Right to a fair election process. Board elections should follow a transparent process with adequate notice and member participation.
Remember: These are general principles. The specific rights available to you depend entirely on your state's statutes and your association's governing documents. Until we publish the full [STATE NAME] guide, always verify your rights against the actual text of your state's law.
