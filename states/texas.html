State Guide — Texas

Texas HOA Member Rights

Texas has one of the largest HOA populations in the nation — and a legal framework that has been significantly expanded by member-rights legislation, most notably SB 1588 in 2021. Here is what Texas law actually requires of your association.

Texas HOA Law Framework

Property Code Chapter 209 — The Primary Residential HOA Law

Texas Property Code Chapter 209 is the primary statute governing residential property owners associations (POAs) in Texas. Note the terminology: Texas law uses "property owners association" (POA) rather than "homeowners association" (HOA), though the terms are functionally interchangeable in everyday use. If your subdivision or community has a mandatory membership association with assessment authority over residential properties, Chapter 209 almost certainly applies.

Chapter 209 was substantially expanded by Senate Bill 1588 in 2021 — one of the most significant pieces of HOA member-rights legislation in Texas history. SB 1588 strengthened open meeting requirements, expanded records access rights, created new due process protections around fines and violations, and imposed new transparency requirements on POA boards. If your association is relying on practices that predate SB 1588, they may no longer be legally valid.

Property Code Chapter 204 — Supplemental Authority

Chapter 204 provides supplemental authority for property owners associations to manage and maintain subdivisions. It is often relevant to older Texas subdivisions that predate more comprehensive HOA legislation. Chapter 204 covers the association's power to levy and collect assessments, enforce deed restrictions, and maintain common areas. Where Chapter 209 and Chapter 204 overlap, Chapter 209 generally prevails.

Property Code Chapter 82 — Condominiums

Texas's Texas Uniform Condominium Act (Chapter 82) governs condominium associations. Like Chapter 209, Chapter 82 provides a framework for association governance, financial management, and member rights — but tailored to the shared ownership structure of condominiums. Condo owners in Texas should be aware that Chapter 82 and Chapter 209 are separate bodies of law, and not all of Chapter 209's protections automatically apply to condominiums.

Deed Restrictions vs. HOA Enforcement

A distinctive feature of Texas HOA law is the significant role that deed restrictions play, separate from HOA rules. Many Texas subdivisions were originally governed by deed restrictions alone — contractual covenants recorded against each property — before formal HOA structures were established. In some communities, deed restrictions and HOA rules coexist, and they can conflict. Understanding which document controls which issue is often the first challenge in a Texas HOA dispute.

Key Texas HOA Member Rights

Right to Inspect Records (§209.005)

Under §209.005, Texas POA members have the right to inspect the association's books and records, including financial statements, meeting minutes, contracts, the list of board members, and the association's governing documents. The association must make records available within 10 business days of a written request.

SB 1588 (2021) strengthened the records access provision by requiring associations to maintain an updated, publicly accessible website (for associations with 60 or more lots) where certain records must be posted — including the governing documents, the most recent financial statement, the annual budget, and the name and contact information for the property management company.

If the association charges a fee for copying records, that fee must be reasonable. The association cannot use fees as a barrier to access. If your records request is denied or ignored, document your request and contact a Texas HOA attorney.

Open Meeting Requirements (§209.0051)

Chapter 209's open meeting requirements apply to residential POAs with 14 or more lots. Under §209.0051, board meetings must be open to members, and the board must provide advance notice of meetings — at least 144 hours (6 days) for regular meetings. The board can go into executive session only for limited purposes: pending litigation, contract negotiations, enforcement of rules, and personnel matters.

Members must be given a reasonable opportunity to speak on agenda items before the board votes. The board cannot conduct business on matters not included in the meeting notice without rescheduling the item. Meeting minutes must be approved and made available to members.

Foreclosure Protections — A Significant Texas Distinction (§209.0092)

Texas HOA foreclosure law is one of the most important protections in the state. Under §209.0092 (added by SB 1588 in 2021), a residential property owners association cannot foreclose on a home for assessment debts unless it obtains a court judgment first — a process called judicial foreclosure. This is a major protection that did not exist under prior Texas law.

Before SB 1588, Texas HOAs could in many cases use a non-judicial foreclosure process — essentially conducting a foreclosure sale without first going to court. This process was widely abused, resulting in Texas homeowners losing their homes over relatively small assessment debts. The 2021 judicial foreclosure requirement creates a meaningful safeguard: the HOA must now sue you in court, give you an opportunity to be heard, and obtain a court order before proceeding to foreclosure.

If your Texas HOA is threatening foreclosure, contact a Texas attorney immediately. The judicial process gives you time to respond, and there are defenses available — including procedural defects in the association's claims process — that can stop or delay foreclosure.

Fair Treatment and Selective Enforcement

Texas law prohibits selective enforcement of deed restrictions and HOA rules — the board cannot enforce rules against you while ignoring the same violations by other members. If you are facing enforcement action for something the association routinely tolerates from other owners, you have a defense. Document the inconsistency: photograph other properties with the same alleged violation, note addresses and dates, and raise selective enforcement at your hearing.

SB 1588 also added new provisions requiring associations to apply standards for architectural review, rule enforcement, and fine assessment in a uniform manner. Inconsistent application of standards is now more clearly a legal vulnerability for Texas associations.

SB 1588 (2021) — The Member Rights Reform Package

Texas SB 1588, passed in the 2021 legislative session, was a comprehensive package of HOA reform measures that addressed many of the most common member complaints. Key provisions beyond what's covered above include:

  • Requiring associations with 60 or more lots to maintain a publicly accessible website with key documents posted
  • Prohibiting associations from preventing the display of political signs (within certain size and time limits before elections)
  • Requiring the board to adopt a written policy on the collection of delinquent assessments
  • Limiting the circumstances in which an association can suspend a member's right to use common areas for assessment delinquency
  • Imposing new restrictions on the ability to tow vehicles from common parking areas without proper notice

Who Oversees Texas HOAs

Texas does not have a dedicated state agency that regulates homeowners associations or handles member complaints the way Florida's DBPR handles condo complaints. There is no HOA complaint hotline at the state level.

The Texas Office of the Attorney General has general consumer protection authority and can investigate HOA practices that rise to the level of deceptive trade practices under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA). However, ordinary governance disputes — fine appeals, records denials, meeting notice violations — are generally beyond the AG's enforcement focus.

Enforcement of Chapter 209 rights is primarily through the Texas district courts. Courts can issue injunctions, award actual damages, and — in cases of knowing violation of Chapter 209 — award attorney's fees to the prevailing party. The attorney's fees provision is an important deterrent but requires you to initiate litigation, which is costly.

For many disputes, a strongly worded certified letter from an attorney citing specific Chapter 209 violations is enough to bring a Texas HOA board into compliance — particularly because SB 1588 made the legal consequences of violations more concrete.

Texas-Specific HOA Issues

"POA" vs. "HOA" Terminology

Texas consistently uses the term "property owners association" (POA) in its statutes, even though most Texas residents use "HOA" in everyday conversation. This distinction can matter when you're searching for your rights in the law — always search Texas statutes for "property owners association" rather than "homeowners association."

Deed Restriction Enforcement by Non-HOA Bodies

In some older Texas neighborhoods, deed restrictions are enforced not by a formal HOA but by a Deed Restriction Enforcement Organization or by individual neighbors suing each other directly. Chapter 209 may not apply in these situations. Understanding the legal form of your community's enforcement mechanism is essential to knowing which statutes govern your rights.

Tax Assessment Interactions

Texas property taxes are among the highest in the country, and some Texas HOA disputes intersect with property tax issues — particularly when associations make improvements to common areas that affect property valuations, or when the HOA's financial mismanagement results in an assessment that is difficult for members to afford given existing tax burdens. These intersections can be complex and often require professional guidance.

Practical Steps for Texas HOA Members

Locate your governing documents. Texas deed restrictions are recorded instruments — you can find them at your county clerk's office or online at your county's property records portal. Request a copy of the Declaration, Bylaws, and Rules from the association.

Check whether your association has a required website. If your HOA has 60 or more lots, SB 1588 requires it to maintain a website with key documents posted. If no website exists, that is itself a violation you can raise.

Make records requests in writing, citing §209.005. Give the association 10 business days to respond. Keep copies of all correspondence. A refusal to produce records is actionable in Texas courts.

Know your foreclosure rights. If you receive a foreclosure notice from your HOA, do not ignore it. Texas law now requires judicial foreclosure, which means the HOA must sue you in court first — you have time to respond and raise defenses. Contact a Texas real estate attorney immediately.

Contact the Texas AG's Consumer Protection Division if you believe the HOA's conduct rises to the level of deceptive or fraudulent practices. While the AG does not typically intervene in individual disputes, a pattern of member complaints about the same association can sometimes trigger interest.
