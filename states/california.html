California HOA Member Rights

California's Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act is the most comprehensive HOA law in the United States — and it gives California homeowners some of the strongest member protections in the country.

The Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act

California's Davis-Stirling Act (Civil Code §§4000–6150) is the comprehensive statute that governs all common interest developments (CIDs) in California — a category that includes planned unit developments, condominium projects, stock cooperatives, and community apartment projects. If you live in a community with shared ownership of common areas and a mandatory membership association, Davis-Stirling applies to you.

Originally enacted in 1985 and substantially reorganized in 2012 (when the code sections were renumbered), Davis-Stirling is routinely updated by the California Legislature. California has one of the most active legislative environments for HOA law in the country — nearly every legislative session includes amendments that expand or clarify member rights. It is worth checking the California Legislative Information website annually to see if any new protections have been added.

Davis-Stirling establishes a legal hierarchy: the Civil Code supersedes your governing documents. Any provision in your CC&Rs or Bylaws that conflicts with Davis-Stirling is unenforceable. This is critically important — boards sometimes attempt to rely on older governing documents that contain provisions the law has since invalidated.

Key Member Rights Under Davis-Stirling

Right to Inspect Records (Civil Code §5200)

California gives members one of the broadest records inspection rights in the country. Under Civil Code §5200, members are entitled to inspect and copy a wide range of association documents, including the governing documents, minutes of board and membership meetings, annual reports, financial statements, budgets, contracts with vendors, and a membership list (in limited circumstances).

The association must respond to a records request within 10 days. If the association denies access to any record, it must state in writing which document it is withholding and why. Blanket refusals are not permitted. If you are denied access to records you're entitled to under §5200, you can pursue enforcement in small claims court for documents valued under the small claims limit, or in superior court for more significant matters — and the court can award attorney's fees to a prevailing member.

Open Meeting Requirements (Civil Code §4900)

California's open meeting law for HOAs is among the strongest in the country. Under Civil Code §4900, all board meetings must be open to members unless the board adjourns to executive session for one of the specifically permitted purposes: litigation, formation of contracts with third parties, member discipline, and personnel matters.

Members must be given an opportunity to speak at meetings before the board takes action on any item. The board cannot take action on items not on the posted agenda — meaning last-minute additions cannot be sprung on members without notice. Meeting minutes must be approved at the next board meeting and distributed to members within a specified time.

HOA Elections — Secret Ballot and Inspector of Elections (Civil Code §5100)

California's election rules under Civil Code §5100 are exceptionally detailed and member-protective. All elections and member votes — including board elections, assessment votes, and votes to amend governing documents — must be conducted by secret ballot. The board cannot simply vote to elect its own members or use a voice vote for member matters.

Associations must appoint an Inspector of Elections — a neutral third party who oversees the election process, validates ballots, and announces results. The Inspector cannot be a board member, a candidate, or an employee of the association. The two-envelope ballot system (outer envelope signed by the voter for identification, inner envelope containing the unmarked ballot for secrecy) must be used. Violation of these election rules is one of the most common grounds for challenging an HOA board election in California.

Internal Dispute Resolution — IDR (Civil Code §5900)

Before you can sue your HOA in court over most disputes, California requires you to attempt Internal Dispute Resolution (IDR) — a meet-and-confer process between the member and a representative of the board. Either party can request IDR. The board is required to participate in good faith. IDR sessions are informal and not binding, but they create a record and sometimes resolve disputes without further escalation.

If IDR fails or if the board refuses to participate, the next step is typically Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR).

Alternative Dispute Resolution — ADR (Civil Code §5925)

California requires parties to participate in ADR — mediation or arbitration — before filing most civil lawsuits related to Davis-Stirling disputes. The requesting party sends a written ADR demand; the responding party must accept or reject within 30 days. If the board rejects a reasonable ADR demand and then loses in court, it may be penalized in attorney's fees.

ADR is particularly important in California because litigation is expensive and slow. Many legitimate HOA disputes — fine disputes, records denials, election irregularities — can be efficiently resolved through mediation if both sides approach it in good faith.

Fine Due Process (Civil Code §5850)

Before your California HOA can fine you, it must: (1) provide prior written notice of the alleged violation, (2) give you an opportunity to be heard at a meeting of the board, and (3) allow you to dispute the violation. The board cannot issue a fine in absentia without following this due process sequence. Fines imposed without proper notice and a hearing opportunity are unenforceable.

Who Oversees HOAs in California

California does not have a single dedicated state agency that regulates HOAs the way that some states (like Arizona) do. There is no HOA complaint hotline where you can report your board and expect an investigation.

The California Department of Real Estate (DRE) has authority related to HOA disclosures in the context of real estate transactions — it enforces the requirement that sellers and associations provide buyers with required disclosures under Davis-Stirling. But the DRE does not regulate ongoing HOA governance or adjudicate member-board disputes.

Enforcement of Davis-Stirling is primarily through the civil courts. This means that if your board is violating your rights, your remedies are IDR, ADR, and ultimately litigation. California courts can issue injunctions, award damages, and — critically — award attorney's fees to a prevailing member in many Davis-Stirling cases. The attorney's fees provision is an important deterrent against board overreach.

California-Specific HOA Issues

Wildfire Liability and Insurance

California's wildfire crisis has created significant complexity for HOAs, especially in the Wildland Urban Interface (WUI). Associations in fire-prone areas may have trouble obtaining or maintaining adequate insurance, which can trigger special assessments, affect property values, and create liability exposure for the board. Members have the right to review the association's current insurance certificate and to understand what the policy covers.

California law requires associations to maintain certain minimum insurance coverage and to disclose coverage details to members annually. If your association's coverage has lapsed or been dramatically reduced, that is a governance issue you can raise at a board meeting and in writing to the board.

ADUs — Accessory Dwelling Units

California state law (Government Code §65852.2) prohibits HOAs from effectively banning ADUs (accessory dwelling units, also known as granny flats or in-law units). State law preempts HOA rules that would prevent a member from building an ADU on their lot where the local zoning allows it. HOAs may impose reasonable design standards but cannot use the CC&Rs as a blanket prohibition. If your HOA is blocking a legally permitted ADU, they are likely violating state law.

Short-Term Rental Restrictions

California HOAs can restrict or ban short-term rentals through properly adopted rules or CC&R provisions. However, any restriction adopted after you purchased your home — through a rule change rather than a CC&R amendment — has a different legal standing than restrictions that were in place when you bought. Retroactive enforcement of new STR restrictions can be legally contested, particularly if you have an established rental history.

How to Find the Davis-Stirling Text

The full text of the Davis-Stirling Act — California Civil Code §§4000 through 6150 — is available for free at the California Legislative Information website: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov. Search for "Civil Code" and navigate to Division 4, Part 5 (Common Interest Developments).

The website also shows you when each section was last amended, which is useful for determining whether your association's governing documents are relying on outdated code provisions.
