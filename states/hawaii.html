Key Hawaii Statutes Governing HOAs

HRS Chapter 421J — Planned Community Associations

Chapter 421J is the primary statute governing homeowners associations in Hawaii's planned communities — developments where individually owned lots share common areas such as roads, parks, pools, or recreational facilities. If you live in a subdivision or master-planned community (not a condominium building), Chapter 421J is the law that governs your HOA.

Chapter 421J was enacted to bring a consistent legal framework to planned community associations across Hawaii. It sets out requirements for how associations must be organized, how they must hold meetings, how they must handle finances, and — critically — what rights members retain regardless of what the CC&Rs say. Any provision in your governing documents that conflicts with Chapter 421J is unenforceable to the extent of the conflict.

HRS Chapter 514B — Condominiums

If you own a condominium unit in Hawaii, your association is governed primarily by Chapter 514B, the Hawaii Condominium Property Act. Chapter 514B is substantially more detailed than 421J and is one of the more comprehensive condo laws in the country. It covers the creation of condominium projects, the rights and obligations of unit owners, the governance of associations, financial management, insurance requirements, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

Chapter 514B was a comprehensive rewrite and modernization of the older Chapter 514A. Many older condominium projects in Hawaii were created under 514A and may still reference it in their governing documents — but Chapter 514B now governs the ongoing operations of virtually all Hawaii condo associations.

HRS Chapter 514A — Older Condominium Projects

Chapter 514A was the original Hawaii condominium law. While Chapter 514B replaced it for most purposes, some provisions of 514A may still apply to condominium projects created before the transition. If your condo documents reference 514A, you should confirm with an attorney which provisions of each chapter apply to your specific association.

HRS Chapter 421H — Nonprofit Corporations

Most Hawaii HOAs are organized as nonprofit corporations. Chapter 421H governs nonprofit corporations in Hawaii and provides the foundational corporate law framework for HOA governance — including rules about boards of directors, member meetings, voting, and organizational structure. When Chapter 421J or 514B is silent on a procedural issue, Chapter 421H often fills the gap.

Who Oversees HOAs in Hawaii

In Hawaii, HOA and condominium associations are overseen by the Real Estate Commission (REC), which operates under the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs (DCCA). The DCCA is Hawaii's primary consumer protection and business regulatory agency, and the Real Estate Commission is the division within it that handles community association matters.

For condominium associations specifically, the DCCA's Real Estate Branch maintains the Condominium Management Education Fund and has authority to investigate complaints against condominium managing agents. It does not typically adjudicate individual disputes between members and boards — its oversight function is more focused on licensing of managing agents and ensuring compliance with Chapter 514B's disclosure and registration requirements.

For planned community HOAs under Chapter 421J, state oversight is more limited. The DCCA does not have the same formal complaint authority over planned community associations that it has over condo associations. This means that for planned community disputes, your primary legal remedy is often civil litigation — or mediation, which Hawaii law strongly encourages before filing suit.

Key Hawaii HOA Member Rights

Right to Inspect Association Records (§421J-15)

Hawaii law gives planned community association members the right to inspect the financial books and records of the association, including meeting minutes, financial statements, budgets, contracts, and governing documents. Under HRS §421J-15, members may request access to these records, and the association is required to make them available during reasonable business hours. The association may charge a reasonable fee for copying but cannot deny access entirely.

For condominium associations, the parallel right is found in HRS §514B-154. Condo owners have the right to inspect the declaration, bylaws, rules, minutes, financial statements, contracts, and other official records. The association must respond within a reasonable time and cannot create barriers designed to discourage inspection.

Practical tip: Always make your records request in writing — by certified mail or email with delivery confirmation — and keep a copy. If the board refuses or ignores your request, the written record is essential if you need to escalate to the DCCA or the courts.

Right to Attend Board Meetings

Hawaii law requires that board meetings of HOAs and condo associations be open to members. The board cannot conduct routine business in secret or exclude members from attending regular board meetings. Members have the right to observe — though not necessarily to speak unless the meeting agenda includes an owner forum period.

The board may go into executive session (closed session) only for specific enumerated purposes — typically personnel matters, pending litigation, contract negotiations where disclosure would harm the association, and similar sensitive matters. The existence of an executive session must be disclosed to members even if the content is not.

Right to Adequate Notice of Meetings

Members must receive at least 10 days advance notice for regular board and membership meetings. For emergency meetings, the board must provide at least 48 hours advance notice. Emergency meetings are supposed to be limited to genuinely urgent matters — they cannot be used to rush through controversial decisions without adequate member notice.

Notice must include the date, time, location, and agenda of the meeting. If a meeting involves a vote on a major issue — such as a special assessment or a significant rule change — the notice should identify that item so members can prepare.

Right to Vote on Major Decisions

Certain decisions require a membership vote — not just a board vote. These typically include amendments to the CC&Rs or Declaration, the adoption of a special assessment above a threshold amount, and changes to the master plan of the development. Your governing documents will specify what vote thresholds apply, but Hawaii law sets minimum floors that governing documents cannot undercut.

Members are entitled to vote in person, by absentee ballot, or by proxy unless the governing documents restrict proxy voting. The association must provide adequate notice of any election or vote, including the specific matter being voted on.

Right to Challenge Assessments and Fines

Hawaii HOA members have the right to due process before the association imposes fines or pursues collections. The board cannot simply levy a fine without providing notice of the alleged violation and an opportunity to be heard. Under Hawaii's community association framework, a member must be given a reasonable opportunity to respond to any alleged violation before a fine is assessed.

If you receive a fine notice, respond in writing within the timeframe specified. Request a hearing before the board. Document everything. An association that skips the due process steps is on weak legal ground if the matter goes to mediation or litigation.

Right to Dispute Resolution and Mediation

Hawaii law strongly encourages — and in some contexts requires — that parties attempt mediation or other alternative dispute resolution (ADR) before filing a lawsuit. The DCCA's Office of Consumer Protection has resources related to dispute resolution, and the Hawaii Mediation Center provides mediation services for community association disputes.

Mediation is not free in most cases, but it is substantially less expensive than litigation. Both parties must agree to participate, and the mediator cannot impose a binding decision — but many disputes are resolved successfully through mediation, especially when both sides have an attorney who can explain the realistic legal landscape.

Hawaii-Specific HOA Issues

Wildfire Risk and Insurance Obligations

The catastrophic 2023 Maui wildfires — one of the deadliest natural disasters in American history — exposed critical gaps in how Hawaii HOAs and condo associations communicate insurance coverage to their members. The core question in post-fire disputes is nearly always: whose insurance covers what?

For condominium associations, Chapter 514B includes provisions about the association's obligation to maintain property and casualty insurance covering the common elements and, depending on the project's declaration, portions of the individual units. However, the line between association-covered property and owner-covered property can be extremely blurry, and governing documents vary enormously.

Every Hawaii condo and HOA member should obtain a copy of their association's insurance certificate, read the declaration carefully to understand the boundary between association and owner responsibility, and purchase their own homeowner's or HO-6 policy to cover the gap. Do not assume the association's policy covers your personal property, your unit's interior improvements, or the cost of alternative housing if the unit becomes uninhabitable.

Tropical Climate and Common Area Maintenance

Hawaii's tropical climate is beautiful — and brutal on building materials and landscaping. HOAs in Hawaii face accelerated deterioration of roofs, siding, fencing, pools, and common area landscaping compared to mainland communities. Members have the right to demand that the board maintain a reserve fund adequate to address predictable major maintenance needs.

If your association is consistently deferring maintenance — or if the reserve fund study has not been updated in years — this is a significant red flag. Deferred maintenance leads to special assessments: large, unexpected charges that can run into the tens of thousands of dollars per unit. Members should ask at every annual meeting for a current reserve fund study and reserve fund balance.

Short-Term Rental Conflicts (Airbnb/VRBO)

Short-term vacation rentals are a major flashpoint in Hawaii's HOA and condo communities. Hawaii counties have enacted various ordinances restricting or regulating short-term rentals (STRs), and many associations have added STR restrictions to their rules or CC&Rs in response to persistent neighbor complaints.

If your association has an STR restriction, enforcement is typically the board's responsibility. If you are being impacted by a neighbor's STR in violation of association rules, you have the right to demand enforcement. Document the violations — screenshots of rental listings, dates of stays, guest arrivals — and submit a written complaint to the board.

Conversely, if your association has adopted new STR restrictions that affect an existing rental practice, check whether the restriction was properly adopted (proper notice, proper vote threshold) and whether it conflicts with any vested rights. These are fact-specific questions that often require an attorney's analysis.

Practical Steps for Hawaii HOA Members

Step 1: Get Your Governing Documents

Your association's governing documents — the Declaration, CC&Rs, Bylaws, and Rules — are the foundation of every HOA dispute. You cannot know your rights or the board's obligations without reading them. Request copies from the board in writing, or check your county Bureau of Conveyances for recorded documents. Hawaii's Bureau of Conveyances maintains an online search portal where recorded documents can be retrieved by property address or legal description.

Step 2: Make a Written Records Request

If you need financial records, meeting minutes, contracts, or other association documents, send a written records request to the board or managing agent. Cite HRS §421J-15 (for planned communities) or §514B-154 (for condominiums). Give the board a specific deadline — 10 business days is reasonable — and state that you will pursue your rights through the DCCA or the courts if the request is not honored.

Step 3: Attend Board Meetings

Regular attendance at board meetings is one of the most powerful tools an HOA member has. It signals to the board that members are paying attention. It allows you to understand financial decisions before they become crises. And it creates a public record — through the minutes — of what was discussed and decided.

Step 4: Explore Mediation Before Litigation

If you have a dispute that cannot be resolved informally, consider mediation through the DCCA or a private mediator before filing in court. Mediation is faster, cheaper, and less adversarial than litigation. Many Hawaii HOA disputes that end up in court could have been resolved in one or two mediation sessions.

Step 5: Consult a Hawaii HOA Attorney

Hawaii's HOA law is complex and fact-specific. If you are facing a significant dispute — a large special assessment, a fine proceeding, a foreclosure threat, or a records denial — consult a Hawaii attorney who practices HOA or community association law. Many attorneys offer a free initial consultation.
