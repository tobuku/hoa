State Guide — Florida

Florida HOA Member Rights

Florida has one of the largest HOA populations in the country — and a legal framework that treats planned communities and condominium associations very differently. Knowing which law applies to your community is the critical first step.

Florida's Two Governing Statutes — And Why the Difference Matters

Chapter 720 — The Florida HOA Act (Planned Communities)

Chapter 720, Florida Statutes governs homeowners associations in Florida's planned communities — subdivisions and developments where individually owned homes or lots share common areas like roads, parks, clubhouses, or amenity centers. If you own a single-family home, townhome, or villa in a community with an HOA and mandatory dues, Chapter 720 is almost certainly your primary law.

Chapter 720 sets the floor for member rights in Florida HOAs. Your governing documents can provide additional protections, but they cannot strip away the rights the statute guarantees. Key among those rights: access to records, open meetings, due process before fines, and protection against arbitrary rule enforcement.

Chapter 718 — The Florida Condominium Act

Chapter 718 governs Florida condominium associations — buildings or communities where unit owners share ownership of common elements (lobbies, hallways, elevators, exterior walls, roofs). Condominiums operate under a more detailed and more heavily regulated framework than planned community HOAs, in part because of the shared structural ownership that makes governance decisions more consequential for all unit owners.

Following the 2021 Surfside condominium collapse that killed 98 people, the Florida Legislature enacted SB 4D (2022) and subsequent legislation substantially strengthening reserve fund requirements and structural inspection obligations for condominium associations. These reforms have significant financial implications for condo owners and boards across Florida.

Key Florida HOA Member Rights

Right to Inspect Official Records (§720.303)

Under §720.303, Florida HOA members have the right to inspect and copy the association's official records. These records include the Declaration, Bylaws, Articles of Incorporation, rules, all contracts, financial records, meeting minutes, and correspondence from association attorneys.

The association must make records available within 10 business days of a written request. Records can be inspected in person or — in many cases — provided electronically. An association that fails to respond within 10 business days is presumed to have denied the request, and the member can pursue remedies including an award of attorney's fees if they prevail in court.

Florida law is unusually specific about records that cannot be withheld — including financial statements, budgets, and written agreements with vendors. Some documents, like personnel records and certain attorney communications, are legitimately protected from disclosure, but the association bears the burden of justifying any redaction.

Open Meetings (§720.303(2))

Board meetings must be open to all members. The board must post notice of meetings at least 48 hours in advance at a conspicuous location within the community, and members must be given an opportunity to speak on agenda items before the board votes. The board may hold closed executive sessions only for limited purposes specified in the statute — primarily pending litigation and attorney-client consultations.

Annual members' meetings must be properly noticed — typically 14 to 60 days in advance, with specific notice provisions for election of board members. Failure to provide proper notice of an election can be grounds for challenging the election results.

Right to Vote and Recall Board Members

Florida HOA members have the statutory right to vote on board elections, amendments to governing documents, and certain other major decisions. Members also have the right to recall board members — even without cause — through a petition signed by a majority of the voting interests. Recall petections can be certified through the DBPR for condominiums, providing a faster path than litigation for removing a board that has lost member confidence.

Fines Due Process — The Fines Committee Requirement (§720.305)

One of the most important and frequently violated provisions in Chapter 720 is the fine due process requirement. Before your Florida HOA can levy a fine against you, the proposed fine must be reviewed and approved by a Fines Committee — an independent committee of at least three members who are not officers, directors, or employees of the association.

The committee must give you reasonable notice and an opportunity to appear before it prior to approving any fine. If the committee does not hold the hearing or does not properly notify you, any fine it approves is unenforceable. This is a genuine procedural protection — not a technicality — and Florida courts take it seriously.

Important: If you receive a fine notice, do not simply pay it if you believe the underlying violation was improper or the process was defective. Review whether the Fines Committee was properly convened and whether you were given proper notice and an opportunity to be heard. An invalid fine should not be allowed to become a collection item on your account.

Estoppel Certificates When Selling (§720.30851)

When you sell a property in a Florida HOA community, the buyer is entitled to an estoppel certificate — a document from the association stating the current amount of assessments owed, any pending special assessments, and any outstanding violations or fines. The association must provide the estoppel certificate within 10 business days of a written request, and it caps the amounts the association can collect at closing.

Estoppel certificates have a 30-day validity period. If the association fails to provide the certificate within 10 business days, it is prohibited from collecting any amount that exceeds what was disclosed in any certificate it previously issued.

Who Oversees Florida HOAs — A Critical Distinction

For Condominiums: Florida DBPR

Florida's Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) — specifically its Division of Florida Condominiums, Timeshares, and Mobile Homes — has regulatory authority over condominium associations. This is a meaningful protection: condo owners can file complaints with the DBPR, which can investigate and in some cases impose penalties or order corrective action.

The DBPR also provides an arbitration program through its Division of Florida Condominiums for many types of condo disputes, which is significantly faster and less expensive than circuit court litigation. For condo owners, this is an important alternative to the courts.

For Planned Community HOAs: The Courts

Florida does not have an equivalent state regulator for planned community HOAs governed by Chapter 720. There is no agency you can file a complaint with that will investigate your HOA board the way the DBPR investigates condo associations. Your primary legal remedy for Chapter 720 violations is the Florida circuit court — which means legal fees, discovery, and a timeline measured in months or years rather than weeks.

This gap in oversight is one of the significant criticisms of Florida HOA law. It means that in planned community disputes, the cost and burden of enforcement falls entirely on individual members. This is why documentation, persistence, and — when necessary — an HOA attorney are so important.

Florida-Specific HOA Issues

Hurricane Preparedness and Reserve Funds

Florida HOAs and condo associations face substantial obligations around hurricane preparedness. Associations are responsible for maintaining common areas — including roofs, exterior structures, and drainage systems — in a state of repair that can withstand storm damage. Reserve funds exist precisely to cover the cost of major repairs without issuing an emergency special assessment.

Post-Surfside reforms enacted in 2022–2023 dramatically strengthened reserve fund requirements for Florida condominiums — particularly buildings three stories or taller. Underfunded reserves are no longer a matter of board discretion; they are now a legal compliance issue with real consequences. If you are a condo owner in Florida, ask your board for the current reserve fund study and reserve fund balance at every annual meeting.

The Surfside Collapse and Structural Inspections

The June 2021 collapse of the Champlain Towers South condominium in Surfside, Florida, which killed 98 people, triggered sweeping legislative changes to Florida condo law. Chapter 718 now requires milestone structural inspections for buildings three stories or taller, beginning at 30 years of age (25 years for buildings within 3 miles of the coast). It also requires structural integrity reserve studies and mandates full funding of reserves for certain structural components.

These requirements represent a significant financial burden on many Florida condo associations — and that burden flows through to unit owners via higher monthly dues or special assessments. But the alternative — ignoring structural deterioration — carries catastrophic risk. If your association is deferring these inspections or fighting the reserve fund requirements, that is a serious red flag about management priorities.

Selective Enforcement and Arbitrary Rule Application

Selective enforcement — where a board applies rules against some members but ignores the same behavior by others — is one of the most common complaints in Florida HOA disputes. Florida courts have held that selective enforcement can be a defense to a fine or violation action. If you are being cited for something the board routinely ignores by other members, document the inconsistency with photographs, dates, and addresses of the other violations, and raise selective enforcement as a defense at your Fines Committee hearing.

Practical Steps for Florida HOA Members

Request records in writing. Always cite §720.303 (HOA) or §718.111 (condo) and give the board a 10-business-day deadline. Keep a copy of your request and all correspondence.

Attend board meetings. Show up. Sign the attendance sheet. Speak during the owner forum. Your presence creates accountability and creates a public record in the minutes.

Fight fines procedurally. If you receive a fine notice, verify that the Fines Committee is properly constituted and that you received proper notice of a hearing. An improperly issued fine is not a valid debt.

For condos — use DBPR. File a complaint with the DBPR at myfloridalicense.com for Chapter 718 violations. The DBPR arbitration program is available for many condo disputes and is far faster than circuit court.

Consult an attorney for major disputes. Fine disputes, records denials, and election challenges are often suitable for self-help with the guidance on this site. But for special assessment challenges, foreclosure threats, or discrimination claims, you need a Florida HOA attorney.
