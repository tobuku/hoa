Where to Search

How to Find an HOA Attorney

These are the most reliable ways to find a qualified HOA attorney who represents homeowners.

🏛️ Community Associations Institute (CAI)

CAI is the national trade organization for community association professionals. Their attorney members are searchable by state. Look specifically for attorneys whose practice focuses on member representation, not board counsel.

Search: "CAI attorney directory" at caionline.org

⚖️ State Bar Referral Services

Every state bar association operates a lawyer referral service. HOA attorneys are typically listed under Real Estate Law or Community Associations. Many offer a free or low-cost initial consultation.

Search: "[your state] bar lawyer referral service"

🔍 Online Legal Directories

Martindale-Hubbell, Avvo, and FindLaw allow you to filter by practice area and location. Search "homeowners association attorney" + your state. Read peer reviews and client reviews separately.

Look for: peer ratings, HOA-specific experience, member-side cases

Before You Hire

Questions to Ask an HOA Attorney

Not all HOA attorneys represent homeowners. Many primarily represent HOA boards and management companies. Ask these questions before committing.

  1. Do you specialize in community association law? HOA law is a distinct specialty. General real estate attorneys may not know your state's HOA statutes well enough.
  2. Do you primarily represent HOA members or HOA boards? An attorney who spends 90% of their time representing boards may have a different perspective than you need.
  3. Have you handled cases similar to mine? Fines disputes, insurance claims, election challenges, and records request violations each have different legal considerations.
  4. What is your experience with [my state's] HOA statutes? State law matters enormously — Hawaii's HRS 421J is very different from California's Davis-Stirling Act.
  5. What are your fees and how are they structured? Hourly vs. flat-fee vs. contingency. Ask for an estimate of total cost for your situation.
  6. Do you offer a free or reduced-cost initial consultation? Many HOA attorneys do. Use it to assess fit before committing.
  7. Does my state have a fee-shifting statute? In some states, if you win an HOA dispute, the HOA may be required to pay your attorney fees. Ask if this applies to your situation.
  8. Have you been before the [state HOA regulatory body or OAH]? Some state agencies handle HOA disputes at lower cost than court. An attorney familiar with these processes can save you money.
